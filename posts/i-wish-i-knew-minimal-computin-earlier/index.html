I Wish I Knew Minimal Computing Earlier

November 20, 2020

It dawned on me just now that I’ve already learnt about the dependencies of the digital world the hard way. Last year, I was working on a sheet music edition. The process was a completely new experience, and I had no idea how to get things done. So I built my workflow from scratch and learnt some bitter lessons about the digital dependencies.

The first step was about scraping all Riga archives to get my hands on every possible edition of the 24 piano preludes by the composer Jānis Medinš. The digital catalogues of the libraries came very handy.

The problems emerged when I had to compare different editions of the same prelude and write down all the differences in a readable way.

Earlier, I had made a few drafts on paper:

Most of the 24 preludes had 2 different editions, some had 3 or even 4. It was a ton of work to 1) find all the differences and 2) write them down in a comparing fashion. To fasten up the process, I created a table in Numbers. I had to figure out how to interpret the musical notation symbols, and – to my joy – Numbers provided a decent amount of beautiful note symbols, which I used non-sparingly:

Joy turned into despair, when I had to send my critical commentary to our editor-in-chief. I copied my Numbers spreadsheet to MSWord (the whole exporting/copying process was a mess), and – what a surprise – it turned out that some of the note symbols where macOS exclusive. What I didn’t have in mind – my colleagues, editing the commentary on Windows, didn’t have a way to work with these symbols:

And so the workflow turned into a nightmare as I had to change all the note symbols into character strings of music nomenclature – for all the 24 preludes. Truth be told, I had to communicate about these issues in due time, I just didn’t know then that these would turn out to be issues. Copy/pasting was mostly impossible due to the inflection of Latvian nouns. On top of that – in the middle of translating all the commentary to English, we realized that, when it comes to musical terms, Americans are more short-spoken than British, thus the quavers will be turned into eighth-notes, demisemiquavers to thirty-second notes (and, yes, hemidemisemiquavers to sixty-fourth notes). But that’s outside the realm of DH. In the end, the new critical edition was published, and I was redeemed of the digital dependency torment.
